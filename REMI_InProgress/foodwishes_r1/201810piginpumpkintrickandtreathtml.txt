<h3 class='post-title entry-title'>
<a href='https://foodwishes.blogspot.com/2018/10/pig-in-pumpkin-trick-and-treat.html'>Pig in a Pumpkin &#8211; Trick and Treat</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content'>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><a href="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-1hAQDEumWZw/W9N8hXWV9PI/AAAAAAAATXs/HxFQwQ3TM7kmSjcijbGj7zskUbs80GXlgCLcBGAs/s1600/IMG_9123.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="433" data-original-width="540" height="256" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-1hAQDEumWZw/W9N8hXWV9PI/AAAAAAAATXs/HxFQwQ3TM7kmSjcijbGj7zskUbs80GXlgCLcBGAs/s320/IMG_9123.JPG" width="320" /></a></span></span></div>
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">Okay, so we&#8217;re not using a whole pig, but we are using a
whole pumpkin, which not only produced some very succulent, flavorful pork, but
also made for an absolutely stunning presentation. This looked so good, that
many people might think it was faked, which really is the ultimate compliment. 
</span></span><br />
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">While this would work in any pumpkin, try to find ones sold
as &#8220;sugar,&#8221; or &#8220;pie&#8221; pumpkins,&#8221; since they have a thicker, sweeter flesh,
compared to the ornamental ones sold for jack-o'-lantering. I believe the variety
I used was called &#8220;cannonball,&#8221; but simply look for round, heavy-feeling
varieties about the size of a volleyball, displayed in the produce department,
and not outside, or in front of the store.</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">As I said in the video, you can season this anyway you want,
but regardless of what exactly goes in your gourd, make sure you roast it until
the meat is tender. How long will depend on the size of course, so be sure to
test the meat as it cooks. The only thing I&#8217;d do differently next time, would
be to pour in a little more cider after a few hours in the oven, since a lot of
it evaporated as it roasted, and I wanted more &#8220;sauce&#8221; when I was done.</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-80BKSYVX8MU/W9N8mlEJYZI/AAAAAAAATXw/kJVFETeRLYkHc8buCxPbeSsbKunHPpUkQCLcBGAs/s1600/IMG_9158.JPG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;"><img border="0" data-original-height="374" data-original-width="540" height="221" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-80BKSYVX8MU/W9N8mlEJYZI/AAAAAAAATXw/kJVFETeRLYkHc8buCxPbeSsbKunHPpUkQCLcBGAs/s320/IMG_9158.JPG" width="320" /></a></span></span></div>
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">After you remove the meat for service, ladle out all the
braising liquid, and let it sit for a few minutes, so you can skim off most of
the fat. Thanks to the little bit of flour on the pork, it should have a nice
thickness, but you can always adjust that with some more roux. Or, simply make
a sauce separately, and then just spike it with your drippings. </span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">Also, if you wanted to add another step, you could use a
blender to make a smoother sauce, but I&#8217;ll leave that up to you. Either way, if
you&#8217;re looking for a fun, and very seasonably appropriate way to cook some pork
shoulder, I couldn&#8217;t think of a better, or more beautiful way, which is why I
really hope you give this a try soon. Enjoy!</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><iframe allow="autoplay; encrypted-media" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/_6YGrmbTWxE" width="560"></iframe></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">Ingredients for one Pig in a Pumpkin:</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1 volleyball-sized cooking pumpkin</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">3 1/2 to 4 pounds of boneless pork shoulder</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1 rounded tablespoon kosher salt (the pumpkin will absorb
some of this) </span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">2 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1/4 teaspoon cayenne pepper</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1/2 teaspoon dried thyme</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1 teaspoon crushed fennel seeds</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1 tablespoon freshly chopped rosemary</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1/3 cup thinly sliced shallots</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">- garlic and sage would have also been great here</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">2 tablespoons flour to coat pork</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">1 tablespoon olive oil for browning meat</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">at least 2 cups hard cider, or more if you can fit it in
before or during cooking</span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><br /></span></span></div>
<div class="MsoNormal">
<span style="font-family: Verdana, sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;">- I roasted mine at 350 F. for about 4 hours</span></span></div>

<p>
So, I commented earlier that I was going to braise my pork in a pot beforehand, then put it in the small pumpkins and just worry about roasting the pumpkins, so it wouldn&#39;t split or collapse. A big change you need to make if braising in a pot beforehand is cut way down on the salt. It was way too salty because it was not leaching in to the flesh of the pumpkin. I put the meat in the pumpkin and used fresh cider instead of the braising liquid during the roasting and that solved the problem. I used the braising liquid later to make soup and gravy, after diluting with various other unsalted liquids. 
</p>
