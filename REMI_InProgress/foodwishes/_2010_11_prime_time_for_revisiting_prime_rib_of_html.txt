<h3 class='post-title entry-title'>
<a href='https://foodwishes.blogspot.com/2010/11/prime-time-for-revisiting-prime-rib-of.html'>Prime Time for Revisiting Prime Rib of Beef</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content'>
<a href="//4.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRAoOlJA0I/AAAAAAAAJZg/T6P7YqMsB8Q/s1600/prime-rib-2.JPG" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}"><img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5545128101052744514" src="//4.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRAoOlJA0I/AAAAAAAAJZg/T6P7YqMsB8Q/s320/prime-rib-2.JPG" style="cursor: pointer; float: left; height: 240px; margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; width: 320px;"></a><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">It's an iconic holiday table scene; you carving a juicy, perfectly pink prime rib while a roomful of friends and family watches, in awe of your awesom</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">eness.<br />
<br />
Unfortunately, despite our best efforts, it's sometimes more like you cutting through a dry, o</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">vercooked r</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">oast while they stare daggers at you.<br />
<br />
You can almost hear them thinking, "Way to screw up $80 worth of beef, jerk. I hope there's lots of gravy."<br />
<br />
</span><a href="//3.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRBGuNucvI/AAAAAAAAJZo/TkfxaYp7kzY/s1600/prime-rib-1.JPG" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}"><img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5545128624940544754" src="//3.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRBGuNucvI/AAAAAAAAJZo/TkfxaYp7kzY/s200/prime-rib-1.JPG" style="cursor: pointer; float: right; height: 150px; margin: 0pt 0pt 10px 10px; width: 200px;"></a><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">Well, hopefully this proven mathematical method will increase your chances for success significantly. This is a new video revisiting the same method I featured in this <a href="http://foodwishes.blogspot.com/2008/12/perfect-prime-rib-of-beef-with.html">Prime Rib post</a> a few</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;"> years ago, which only used photos. There are lots of great comments on the original post, and if you're skeptical, you should <a href="http://foodwishes.blogspot.com/2008/12/perfect-prime-rib-of-beef-with.html">go check them out</a>.<br />
<br />
</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">Here is the formula for what was called, "Method X." The rib is brought to room temperature. Overnight is good, but at least </span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">6 hours (this is</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;"> CRITICAL)! Season anyway you like. Then multiply the exact weight times 5 minutes. For me it was 5.35 x 5 = 26.75 minutes, which we round up to 27.<br />
<br />
</span><a href="//1.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRBV9OHvoI/AAAAAAAAJZw/67bGpMqIq38/s1600/prime-rib.JPG" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}"><img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5545128886666772098" src="//1.bp.blogspot.com/_TtjHZtf6vtA/TPRBV9OHvoI/AAAAAAAAJZw/67bGpMqIq38/s200/prime-rib.JPG" style="cursor: pointer; float: left; height: 150px; margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; width: 200px;"></a><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">The rib is cooked at 500 degrees F for exactly that many minutes. Then the oven is turned off. You wait 2 hours without opening the door. You then remove the prime rib and slice into the most perfectly medium-rare meat you've ever seen. By the way, I will be posting a short how-to for a quick au jus soon. Enjoy!<br />
<br />
<br />
</span><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;"><span style="font-size: 130%;">Special Notes: </span><br />
</span><br />
<ul>
<li><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">To use this method you must have a full-sized, modern oven. It must have a digital temperature setting that indicates when it is preheated. Older ovens with manual controls can vary greatly, and the doors may not have the proper insulation. </span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">I've heard from lots of people that have used electric ovens and reported great results. </span></li>
<li><span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;">This is a specific formula for achieving a perfectly pink prime rib cooked somewhere a shade under medium rare. I have no info on altering it for other degrees of doneness. </span></li>
</ul>
<span style="font-family: verdana; font-size: 85%;"><span style="color: #cc0000;"><b>IMPORTANT PRO TIP!!!: </b></span>Prime rib is very expensive, so no matter what method you use (traditional or Method X), you should always have a probe-style thermometer inserted so that the internal temp can be monitored, to avoid any chance of over-cooking. Set the probe alarm (125 F. for medium-rare) just in case, and pull the roast from oven even if there's still time left on the timer.&nbsp;</span><br />

4 to 8 pound Prime Rib of Beef, bone-in, fat cap removed (ask the butcher to explain)<br />
kosher salt as needed<br />
1/4 cup soft butter<br />
1 tablespoon freshly ground black pepper<br />
1 teaspoon Herbes de Provence (this is just a dried herb blend - you can use any thing you like, or just salt and pepper)<br />
